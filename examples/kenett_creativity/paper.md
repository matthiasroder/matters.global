# From Structure to Dynamics: A Network Science of Creative Search Across Minds and Machines

**A synthesis perspective.**

> *Provenance.* This manuscript was synthesized from a dependency graph of 750 research "matters" and 1,522 prerequisite relations, automatically extracted (large-language-model extraction) from the abstracts of 100 papers by Y. N. Kenett and collaborators and then enriched with a second cross-corpus dependency pass. It is a structured reading of that program, not a peer-reviewed report, and contains no new empirical data. In-text citations are author–date and link to the References list; all cited works are from the analyzed corpus. Claims should be verified against the primary sources before citation.

## Abstract

Over fifteen years, a coherent research program has reframed creativity as a property of how semantic memory is organized and traversed. Representing memory as a network, and creativity as a function of that network's structure and the search dynamics over it, this program has produced a measurement substrate (free association, individually estimated semantic networks, semantic distance, forward flow, oscillatory markers), a structural signature of the creative mind (more flexible, cohesive, and less rigid networks), and a neural account linking that structure to brain network dynamics and, ultimately, to clinical variation. Reading the program as a dependency graph makes its center of gravity visible — and shows that its frontier is no longer *structure* but *dynamics*: how the semantic network is searched, switched, and controlled over time. We argue that this dynamical, control-theoretic turn provides a single lens that now generalizes across agents and populations — human and machine creativity, the developing and aging mind, and the front-end act of question-asking. We close with a forward agenda and a methodological call: to make process-level measurement rigorous and shared, and to counter the field's fragmentation by standardizing the instruments this program already owns.

## 1. The network turn in creativity science

Creativity has long resisted measurement, in part because its central construct — the generation of ideas that are both novel and useful — sits downstream of processes that are hard to observe directly ([Green et al., 2023](#ref59); [Kenett et al., 2020c](#ref24)). The decisive move of the last decade has been to stop treating creativity as a trait to be scored and to start treating it as an *emergent property of a cognitive system* that can be represented formally. That system is semantic memory, and the formalism is the network: concepts as nodes, associations as edges, and creative thought as a walk over the resulting structure ([Hills & Kenett, 2021](#ref34); [Kenett & Faust, 2019](#ref13); [Siew et al., 2019](#ref8)).

This network turn did two things at once. It gave creativity a quantitative object of study — the topology of an individual's semantic memory — and it imported a mature analytic toolkit from network science: measures of distance, clustering, modularity, and robustness; models of spreading activation and search; and, more recently, the machinery of network control ([Kenett et al., 2025](#ref88); [Siew et al., 2019](#ref8)). The program reviewed here is the most sustained development of that turn. Its earliest matters concern the structure of the mental lexicon itself ([Kenett et al., 2011](#ref1)) and the structure of semantic networks in more versus less creative individuals ([Kenett et al., 2014](#ref3)); everything else builds on those roots.

## 2. The measurement substrate

A theory of creativity-as-network-property is only as good as its ability to estimate the network. The foundational layer of the program is therefore methodological, and in the dependency graph these method matters are the most generative — they are prerequisites for the largest number of downstream claims.

The taproot is the **free-association paradigm**, used as a window onto semantic memory structure, together with procedures for turning association data into individually estimated networks ([Christensen & Kenett, 2019](#ref17)). From this base grew a family of instruments: relatedness-judgment and association-cloud methods for individual network construction; **semantic distance** as a continuous index of how far apart two ideas sit in semantic space, validated and automated across languages ([Kenett, 2019](#ref19); [Patterson et al., 2023](#ref56); [Reilly et al., 2023](#ref53)); and **forward flow**, a metric that quantifies how far a stream of free thought travels over time and predicts creative achievement ([Gray et al., 2019](#ref11); [Kenett et al., 2020a](#ref21)). A parallel, neurally grounded measurement line identifies **oscillatory markers** — notably the role of alpha oscillations in free and goal-directed semantic association ([Zioga et al., 2024](#ref76)). The program has been explicit that this measurement work is itself the contribution: a neurally informed ontology of *what creativity measurement should even target* ([Kenett et al., 2020c](#ref24)).

Two features of this substrate matter for what follows. First, it is individual-level: the unit of analysis is one person's estimated network, which makes individual differences — and, later, individual interventions — tractable. Second, it is increasingly automated and language-general ([Patterson et al., 2023](#ref56); [Reilly et al., 2023](#ref53)), which is precisely what makes the later comparison between human and machine creativity possible on a common footing.

## 3. The structural signature of the creative mind

With networks estimable, the program established its central empirical claim: **the structure of semantic memory drives creative ability**. More creative individuals have semantic networks that are more flexible, more cohesive, and less rigidly fragmented; less creative individuals have networks that are stiffer and more modular ([Kenett et al., 2014](#ref3)). This signature has been replicated and refined across tasks and dimensions — relating semantic-memory structure to both verbal and figural creativity via associative ability ([He et al., 2020](#ref28)), to the production of creative metaphor through flexible structure ([Li et al., 2021](#ref33)), and to the role of connector hubs that bridge otherwise separate regions of the network ([He et al., 2025](#ref66)).

The relationship is not a simple "more connectivity is better." A recurring theme is the **cost–benefit structure** of semantic memory: richer, denser memory can supply more raw material for ideas while simultaneously constraining their originality, producing a fan-like trade-off between idea quantity and quality ([Beaty et al., 2019](#ref12); [Beaty et al., 2022](#ref46)). This nuance — that the *organization*, not merely the *amount*, of knowledge governs creative output — is what links the structural program to education and expertise, where knowledge acquisition is shown to reshape semantic memory and, with it, creative thinking ([Denervaud et al., 2021](#ref32); [Kenett, 2024](#ref75)).

## 4. From structure to dynamics: search, switching, and control

If the program's foundation is structural, its frontier is dynamical. A static network is a map; creativity is the *journey* across it. The dependency graph's deepest arcs run from measurement primitives, through behavioral mechanism, to brain dynamics — and the matters at the leading edge are almost all about process rather than shape.

Three dynamical constructs anchor this shift. The first is **semantic search**: creative ideation is increasingly modeled as a search over the semantic network, with the relevant variable being not where the network is dense but how it is traversed — the balance of broad exploration against focused exploitation, and the cadence of switching between the two ([Ovando-Tellez et al., 2022a](#ref40); [Ovando-Tellez et al., 2025](#ref94)). The "switching, fast and slow" account makes the dynamics explicit, tying memory-search behavior to its brain-connectivity signature and to creative ability ([Ovando-Tellez et al., 2025](#ref94)). The second is a **formal theory of exploration**: an entropy-modulation account that casts creative exploration as the regulated injection and damping of variability during search ([Hills & Kenett, 2025](#ref62)). The third, and most consequential, is **control**: network control theory, applied to structural brain networks, asks which states the system can be driven toward and at what cost, and links controllability to creativity and intelligence ([Kenett et al., 2018b](#ref9)).

Together these reframe the central question. It is no longer "what does the creative network look like?" but "how is the creative network searched, and how can that search be steered?" This is the pivot on which the rest of the program's generalizations turn, and — as the program itself notes in its calls for process-focused assessment ([Green et al., 2023](#ref59)) — it is where measurement still lags behind theory.

## 5. The neural architecture and its perturbation

The dynamical account is grounded in brain network neuroscience. Creative generation reconfigures semantic memory networks in real time ([Kenett & Thompson-Schill, 2020](#ref25)), and is supported by the default-mode network's contributions to self-generated and episodic-semantic processing during divergent thinking ([Beaty et al., 2020](#ref16); [Kenett et al., 2020b](#ref22)), with electrophysiological evidence assigning the default-mode network a causal role ([Bartoli et al., 2024](#ref68)). Crucially, it is the *dynamic interaction* between large-scale systems — and the ability to switch between them — that predicts creative ability, not the activity of any single region ([Chen et al., 2025](#ref86); [Kenett et al., 2024](#ref71)). Connectome-based predictive modeling closes the loop back to behavior and even to real-world outcomes, with brain connectivity predicting real-life creativity in a manner mediated by semantic-memory structure ([Herault et al., 2024](#ref67); [Ovando-Tellez et al., 2022b](#ref41); [Ovando-Tellez et al., 2023](#ref54)) — a result that ties the neural, structural, and behavioral strands into one chain.

Two moves give this neural account unusual leverage. The first is **perturbation**: if creativity reflects steerable network dynamics, it should be manipulable. Non-invasive prefrontal stimulation alters the novelty of generated ideas ([Kenett et al., 2021](#ref35)), and even simulated perceptual manipulations can shift cognitive flexibility ([Rastelli et al., 2022](#ref37)), turning a correlational structure–creativity link toward a causal one. The second is **clinical extension**: the same control-theoretic and network-dynamic tools transfer to atypical states — a network control theory analysis of depression symptoms ([Kenett et al., 2018a](#ref7)), the consequences of semantic-memory degeneration for creative thinking ([Paulin et al., 2020](#ref27)), and disrupted semantic flow in aphasic narrative production ([Litovsky et al., 2022](#ref47)). The program's deepest single dependency arc runs precisely this route, from an oscillatory measurement method all the way to specific neural substrates of subclinical depression — a reminder that the creativity framework doubles as a general account of semantic-system health.

## 6. Generalization I: minds and machines

The most rapidly growing region of the graph concerns artificial agents, and it is here that the program's measurement substrate pays an unexpected dividend. Because the lab already owns language-general, automated metrics of creative output (semantic distance, forward flow, originality indices), those same instruments can be turned on large language models. The emerging finding is striking: model creative output is **homogeneous** — both narrower than human output within a model and, more surprisingly, convergent *across* different models, a distinct phenomenon from single-model narrowing ([Wenger & Kenett, 2025](#ref95); [Wenger & Kenett, 2026](#ref98)). Cognitive-network methods extend this to higher-level constructs, comparing the "mindsets" of human and model-simulated experts ([Haim et al., 2025](#ref81)), and the cautionary methodological point is made directly: using cognitive psychology to understand model behavior requires going beyond human biases rather than assuming them ([Stella et al., 2023](#ref61)).

This line is not merely diagnostic. It motivates **cyber-creativity** as a research construct in its own right — a decalogue of challenges for human–AI creative collaboration ([Corazza et al., 2025](#ref85)) — and concrete tools, such as AI systems for personalized creativity enhancement ([Kreisberg-Nitzav & Kenett, 2025](#ref82)) and automatic originality assessment used to predict the impact of scientific work itself ([Culbert et al., 2025a](#ref87); [Culbert et al., 2025b](#ref89)). The program is, in effect, proposing its instruments as a *common yardstick* for creativity wherever it occurs, biological or artificial, and asking why machine creativity collapses toward a shared mode — a question its semantic-space framework is unusually well-posed to answer.

## 7. Generalization II: development, aging, and inquiry

The same dynamical lens generalizes across the lifespan and into the act of inquiry that precedes idea generation.

**Across the lifespan**, semantic networks change in estimable ways with age and knowledge: their resilience and structure differ across development and aging ([Cosgrove et al., 2021](#ref36); [Cosgrove et al., 2023](#ref51); [Teles et al., 2025](#ref69)), and abstract, semantically diverse vocabulary helps preserve network resilience later in life ([Cosgrove et al., 2026](#ref91)). A recurring recent result reframes creativity as a form of **cognitive reserve** — associated with the preservation of semantic-memory flexibility and well-being in older adulthood, a "fountain of youth" relationship between creative ability and semantic network health ([Campidelli et al., 2026](#ref84); [Cosgrove et al., 2025](#ref79)). This turns the structural program toward translation: network-based markers of creative aging, and creativity training as a candidate reserve-building intervention.

**Inquiry** is the other frontier. A fast-growing cluster treats **question-asking and curiosity** as the exploratory front-end of creativity — the search for *what to ask* that precedes the search for *what to answer*. Grounded theoretically in the memory and reward systems that support curiosity, creativity, and aesthetics ([Kenett et al., 2023](#ref49)), this work has built empirical engines no one else has: analyses of online question games ([Sasson & Kenett, 2023](#ref48)), and purpose-built platforms — "Spot the Spy" ([Sasson-Lazovsky et al., 2025](#ref92)) and "The Martian Game" ([Cohen et al., 2026](#ref100)) — for studying open-ended question-asking in the wild, complemented by text-mining of question-asking in creativity research ([Wròblewska et al., 2025](#ref78)). The conceptual throughline, from question-asking to prompt engineering ([Sasson Lazovsky et al., 2024](#ref74)) and from knowledge to inquiry ([Kenett, 2024](#ref75); [Raz & Kenett, 2026](#ref97)), positions question-asking as the natural complement to idea generation within the same search framework.

## 8. A forward agenda

Read as a whole, the graph points to four directions and one methodological commitment.

1. **A control-theoretic account of creative search.** Combine individually estimated semantic (and structural-brain) networks with network control theory and explicit search models ([Hills & Kenett, 2025](#ref62); [Kenett et al., 2018b](#ref9); [Ovando-Tellez et al., 2025](#ref94)) to predict *who can be steered* toward more flexible search, then test those predictions against stimulation and training manipulations ([Kenett et al., 2021](#ref35); [Kreisberg-Nitzav & Kenett, 2025](#ref82)). This is the move that converts the program's correlational structure–creativity link into an individualized, causal one, and it directly answers the standing call for process-focused assessment ([Green et al., 2023](#ref59)).

2. **Cyber-creativity as a shared measurement program.** Establish the lab's metrics as the common instrument for human and machine creativity, and develop a semantic-space account of *why* model creativity is homogeneous ([Haim et al., 2025](#ref81); [Wenger & Kenett, 2025](#ref95); [Wenger & Kenett, 2026](#ref98)), pairing diagnosis with the design of collaborations that measurably restore diversity ([Corazza et al., 2025](#ref85); [Kreisberg-Nitzav & Kenett, 2025](#ref82)).

3. **Question-asking as creativity's exploratory front-end.** Consolidate the scattered question-asking findings and platforms ([Cohen et al., 2026](#ref100); [Raz & Kenett, 2026](#ref97); [Sasson & Kenett, 2023](#ref48); [Sasson Lazovsky et al., 2024](#ref74); [Sasson-Lazovsky et al., 2025](#ref92)) into a single theory linking inquiry to semantic-network navigation and knowledge growth.

4. **Creativity as cognitive reserve.** Pursue longitudinal, network-based markers of creative aging and test creativity as a reserve-building intervention ([Campidelli et al., 2026](#ref84); [Cosgrove et al., 2025](#ref79); [Cosgrove et al., 2026](#ref91)).

The cross-cutting commitment is **methodological**. Every direction above depends on dynamic, process-level measures — search trajectories, switching, controllability, semantic flow — rather than static structure; making those measures rigorous and shareable is the contribution the program's own open problems demand ([Green et al., 2023](#ref59)). And the field has named its central risk — fragmentation, and the absence of consensus ([Ross et al., 2026](#ref96)). The program is uniquely positioned to counter it by setting standards: open pipelines for network estimation ([Christensen & Kenett, 2019](#ref17)), shared tasks, and benchmarks that apply equally to human and machine creativity ([Kenett et al., 2025](#ref88); [Ross et al., 2026](#ref96)). Owning the measurement substrate is how a productive program becomes a field.

## 9. Conclusion

The structural foundation of a network science of creativity is essentially built: semantic memory, represented as an individually estimable network, is an engine of creative thought, and its organization — flexible, cohesive, navigable — is the signature of the creative mind. The frontier is no longer that structure but its **dynamics**: how the network is searched, switched, and controlled, in brains and, increasingly, in machines. A single dynamical, control-theoretic lens now unifies what had been separate literatures — neural mechanism, clinical variation, machine creativity, the aging mind, and the act of inquiry. The next phase of the program is to make that lens causal, cross-agent, and standardized.

## Methods and provenance

The analysis underlying this perspective was produced as follows. The full publication list of the focal author was retrieved from Semantic Scholar; the 102 records with abstracts were passed to a large-language-model extractor that, for each abstract, proposed candidate "matters" (claims, findings, contributions, methods, problems, and open questions) with observable resolution conditions, plus dependency candidates (prerequisite relations) against the matters extracted so far. Candidates were de-duplicated into a single state of 750 matters; dependencies whose endpoints both existed were retained, and a second pass showed the model the full matter inventory and solicited additional cross-corpus prerequisite edges, each validated for valid identifiers, non-duplication, and acyclicity before insertion (980 → 1,522 edges). Structural readings in this paper — most-generative foundations (by transitive reach), hubs (by degree), and the deepest dependency arc (longest path in the directed acyclic graph) — were computed directly on the resulting graph.

Three caveats bound the claims. The extraction operated on abstracts, not full texts, so fine-grained results and boundary conditions are not represented. Matters and their dependencies are model-generated and reflect what the abstracts assert, which may diverge from the primary findings. And authorship of individual works is abbreviated here to the corpus level; the References list gives titles, years, and DOIs for verification.
