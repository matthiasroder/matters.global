# Research Directions for the Kenett Lab: A Dynamic, Cross-Agent Science of Creative Search

## 1. Where the program stands

The graph describes one unusually coherent program. Its densest knot is a four-way loop: **creativity ↔ semantic networks (479 dependency links), ↔ semantic memory (368), ↔ measurement (331), ↔ the brain (250).** The highest-degree matters are the load-bearing claims you have spent a decade establishing — *semantic memory structure as a driver of creativity* (the single most-connected node), *high-creative semantic networks are more cohesive and flexible*, *individual-based semantic network construction*, *associative thinking as a core mechanism*, and the measurement layer (*forward flow*, *semantic distance*, *network connectivity/structure as creativity indices*). In short: a network theory of semantic memory, an individual-network methodology to operationalize it, creativity as the explanatory target, and a computational measurement stack tying them together.

What is striking is the **center of mass in time**: matters per publication year climb from 28 (2018) to 109 (2023), 115 (2024), and 152 (2025). Two-thirds of the program, by this measure, is from the last three years, and three new fronts have opened that are not yet fully wired into the core: **AI/LLMs**, **question-asking & curiosity**, and **lifespan/clinical translation**. These are the obvious places to push.

## 2. The throughline: from structure to controllable dynamics

The graph also names its own frontier. The open-question cluster repeatedly flags the same gap — *structure–process interaction in cognitive network models*, the *process definition of creativity*, a *call for process-focused creativity assessments*, and *semantic distance examined only indirectly due to measurement challenges*. Meanwhile the newest mechanistic matters are all dynamical: *MVT as a framework for semantic memory search*, *exploration–exploitation trade-off as a core feature of creative search*, *entropy-modulation theory of creative exploration*, *DMN–ECN switching as a neural predictor*, *mental navigation as a common mechanism for intelligence, creativity, and openness*, and *network control theory (NCT) applied to creativity*.

The unifying move for the next phase is to shift the object of study **from the shape of the semantic network to how it is searched, controlled, and steered over time** — and to run that single process lens across every agent and population the lab touches: human, machine, developing, aging, and clinical. The four directions below are that thesis made concrete.

## 3. Four directions

**D1 — A control-theoretic account of creative semantic search.** You have the structure (individual networks), candidate dynamics (MVT/ACS search, switching, exploration–exploitation), a control formalism (*NCT applied to creativity via DTI*, *distinct NCT profiles for creativity facets*), and intervention handles (tDCS, *Creativeable*). Combine them: use network-control metrics on individual semantic (and structural-brain) networks to *predict who can be steered toward more flexible search*, then test that prediction against a stimulation/training manipulation. This converts the lab's descriptive structure–creativity link into a causal, individualized one and directly closes the structure→process gap the graph flags.

**D2 — Cyber-creativity: one measurement program for humans and machines.** The 2025–2026 frontier is dense here — *LLM creative outputs are more homogeneous than humans'*, *cross-LLM homogeneity as a phenomenon distinct from single-LLM narrowing*, *standardized creativity tasks as evaluation for LLM diversity*, *cyber-creativity as a unifying human–AI construct*, *alignment of AI creative systems to human values*. The lab is uniquely positioned because it already owns the yardsticks (semantic distance, forward flow, DSI, individual networks). Direction: establish the lab's instruments as the **common metric** for human and machine creativity, characterize *why* model creativity collapses toward a shared mode (a network/semantic-space account of homogeneity), and design human–AI collaborations that measurably restore diversity. This is the most fundable and field-defining of the four.

**D3 — Question-asking as the missing front-end of creativity.** This cluster is growing fast but still under-connected (68 matters across 15 papers, only ~89 links into creativity). The recent matters — *question asking as a cognitive mechanism for creative ideation*, *domain-specific question asking improves with knowledge*, *knowledge reshapes inquiry* — plus the game platforms (*Spot the Spy*, *The Martian Game*, the *Akinator* analysis) give you an empirical engine no one else has. Direction: formalize question-asking as the **exploratory phase** of creative search (the complement to idea generation), tie it to semantic-network navigation and knowledge growth, and consolidate the scattered findings into a single theory. This is open white space the lab is already pioneering.

**D4 — Creativity as cognitive reserve across the lifespan.** A recurring 2025 result is *creativity as a protective factor* — for *semantic-memory flexibility in aging* and for *cognitive well-being and independence in older adults* — alongside *age-related differences in individual networks* and *semantic-memory degeneration*. Direction: longitudinal, network-based markers of creative aging, and a test of creativity training as a reserve-building intervention. This gives the program a translational, health-funded arm without leaving the network/semantic-memory core.

## 4. Two cross-cutting commitments

**Lead on process-focused measurement.** Every direction above depends on dynamic measures (search trajectories, switching, controllability, semantic flow) rather than static structure. Making those measures rigorous and shareable *is* the contribution the open-question cluster is asking for.

**Counter fragmentation by setting the standard.** The graph contains an explicit warning — *fragmentation of creative cognition research as a critical barrier to consensus*. The lab's tooling (SemNA, the relatedness-judgment individual-network task, forward flow, DSI) makes it the natural standard-bearer: publish open pipelines and shared benchmarks for both human and LLM creativity. Owning the measurement substrate is how a lab turns a productive program into a field.

## 5. Synthesis

The next three to five years write themselves out of this graph: **a dynamic, control-theoretic, cross-agent science of creative search**, with the lab's measurement stack as the shared instrument and three application arenas — machine creativity (D2), lifespan/clinical reserve (D4), and education via question-asking (D3) — all resting on the same process-level account of how semantic memory is navigated and steered (D1). The structural foundation is essentially built; the frontier is dynamics, causation, and generalization across agents.
